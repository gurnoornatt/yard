## Part 1: What we're doing and why

### The agency

**What we are (the category we file ourselves under):** an underwriting-acceleration and acquisition decision-support service for multifamily syndicators. We are explicitly NOT a "deal-flow agency" or "sourcing vendor" — that label triggers the buyer's "I've heard this, I source through relationships" reflex and gets filed mentally with noise before we've said anything specific. Four independent reviews of this plan converged on the same finding: the sourcing label scores ~5.5/10 (commodity, saturated), the analyst-acceleration framing scores ~8.5/10 (real, provable pain). We adopt the high-scoring identity.

A one-person productized service. Two deliverables, one retainer, in priority order:

1. **OM analysis (the core product)** — forward up to 4 broker OMs per month, get back a structured decision-support analysis within 48-72 hours. Cuts the 2-3 hours an analyst spends on public-records due diligence per deal down to minutes. This is what they renew for.
2. **Weekly list (the wedge)** — 5 pre-broker acquisition candidates in their submarket every week, scored on public-records signals. This is what gets them in the door, not the main event. Framed as "owners before they reach a broker," never "forced sellers" (commodity language).

Continuous service, $5K setup + $4K/mo retainer, 6-month minimum. The premium price is justified by saved analyst hours and better decisions, not by vague off-market promises.

### Who we sell to

Managing partners of multifamily syndication firms, 3-15 employees, $20M-$300M AUM, Sun Belt focused. They have plenty of deal flow from brokers; what they lack is (a) faster triage on the broker deals they're already evaluating, and (b) intelligence on owners before brokers circulate them. Note the ordering: triage is the primary, provable pain; pre-broker sourcing is secondary support.

### The pain we solve, honestly

**Sourcing pain (weekly list):** Brokers represent sellers. They call our prospect when an owner has already decided to sell. Our prospect sees the same deal as 20 other buyers, with price discovery already started. There's a gap of 6-9 months between when an owner becomes a forced seller and when a broker signs them. That's where the weekly list lives.

**Triage pain (OM analysis):** Our prospect gets 30-50 broker OMs per month. Each takes 2-4 hours of analyst time to properly underwrite. Most are noise. Analyst capacity is the actual bottleneck, not OM volume. That's where the OM analysis lives.

**Critical framing — we make analysts more powerful, we do not replace them.** This is non-negotiable positioning. The analyst is frequently the person who evaluates vendors and influences the buying decision. A pitch that threatens their job gets killed internally before it reaches the managing partner. Our product is a force multiplier for the analyst: it does the 2-3 hours of public-records grunt work (pulling ownership chains, deed filings, permits, comps, violations) so the analyst spends their time on judgment, underwriting nuance, and deal strategy — the high-value work they were hired for. We are the analyst's research assistant, not their replacement. Every piece of outbound messaging, the landing page, the discovery-call script, and the OM deliverable framing must reinforce "your analyst, supercharged" and must never imply "you can let your analyst go." The emotional frame is positive (a gift to the team) not negative (a threat to a job).

### Why the hybrid offer beats either alone

The weekly list alone gets us in the door but doesn't address the "I already get deal flow" objection. The OM analysis alone is a research service, not a product — and you can't pitch it cold because the prospect has no reason to forward you anything.

Together: the weekly list is the wedge (reason to engage), the OM analysis is the stickiness (reason to renew). The weekly list teaches us their buy box. The OM analysis monetizes our ongoing relationship. Both share the same underlying data infrastructure.

### Strategic evolution (planning principle, not marketing change)

The weekly list is the marketing wedge. The OM analysis is the durable business. When the CMBS maturity wall passes in 2027 and "forced sellers" loses urgency, the OM analysis stays valuable forever — buyers always want second opinions on broker deals. We don't telegraph this pivot. We let the product evolve into it.

### Why us — honestly

We're not categorically better than CRED iQ or Actovia. Our edge is **format and tier**:
- CRED iQ sells CMBS data to institutional buyers with analysts
- Actovia sells a database subscription, contact data thin below 10-property owners
- Reonomy has scale but bad data quality in our submarkets

We sell curated answers, not databases. We cover the sub-CMBS tier (1-5 property owners). We provide structured second opinions on broker deals nobody else does.

That edge is narrow and time-limited. Defensible for 12-24 months while the maturity wall unwinds. We don't need a forever moat.

### Why this is now and not later

- 2026 CMBS maturity wall: $146B in hard maturities, peak Q4 2026
- Multifamily-specific maturity wall: $539B in 2026, $550B in 2027
- Failed syndicator cohort had $1.7B in floating-rate debt maturing by end of 2025

18-month window to extract value, then the macro thins.

## Part 7: The signal model

Five signals, weighted:

1. **Loan maturity (weight 4)** — Owner has non-CMBS loan maturing in next 12-18 months. Strongest signal.
2. **Hold period (weight 3)** — Owner held ≥ 7 years.
3. **Out-of-state owner (weight 2)** — Owner mailing address not in property's state.
4. **Recent renovation permit (weight 2)** — Non-new-construction permit in last 24 months.
5. **Entity ownership (weight 1)** — LLC, LP, Trust, Inc, Corp.

Composite score 1-10. Properties scoring 7+ go on the candidate list.

### Filter out broker-covered properties

- Currently publicly listed (LoopNet, Crexi) — excluded
- Failed-syndicator portfolios (Tides, GVA, Lurin, WindMass) — excluded
- 10+ property portfolios — excluded (institutional brokers cover)
- CMBS loans — de-prioritized (CRED iQ covers these)
- Regional bank / debt fund loans — included (broker-blind tier)

This is what makes us not noise in their inbox.

## Part 17: Competitive positioning — why we're different enough to warrant $4K/month

This section exists to answer the recurring fear: "Is this a real $5K pain, and are we actually different from what exists?"

### The competitive landscape, by category

**Marketplaces (LoopNet, Crexi, Apartments.com).**
LoopNet is the world's #1 CRE marketplace — 300K+ active listings, 13M+ monthly visitors, $380B+ in transaction value, owned by CoStar Group. Its entire value proposition is *maximum exposure*: sellers list there so the largest possible buyer pool sees the property.

This is not our competitor. It is the proof of our pain. By definition, anything on LoopNet has been seen by every syndicator in the market, bid up, and stripped of margin. A seller who lists on LoopNet has already surrendered negotiating leverage. Our entire thesis — "the deals worth having never reach a marketplace" — is validated by LoopNet's existence and scale. Every syndicator already knows the LoopNet inventory is picked-over. Their hunt for off-market deals IS the $5K pain. LoopNet being a multi-billion-dollar business is the market telling us the public channel is saturated.

Positioning line for discovery calls: "By the time it's on LoopNet, you've already lost. We work the other side of that — owners before they'd ever list."

**Databases (CoStar, Reonomy, CRED iQ, Actovia).**
These sell raw data access. The buyer logs in, learns the tool, runs queries, interprets results, and does the work. CRED iQ has a $2T transaction database but sells to institutional buyers with analyst teams. Reonomy has scale but documented data-quality problems in smaller markets. Actovia's contact data thins out below 10-property owners.

We are not a database. We are a curated answer. The difference is a gym membership versus a personal trainer. They sell access; we sell 5 properties and an OM verdict, with the work already done. The syndicator learns nothing, queries nothing, interprets nothing.

**Brokers (CBRE, Berkadia, Marcus & Millichap, JLL, Northmarq).**
Brokers represent sellers who have already decided to sell. By the time a broker calls our prospect, the owner has set expectations, the broker has 20 likely buyers, and price discovery has begun. Brokers also don't prioritize owners with 1-5 properties — too small to justify relationship-building.

We feed the broker channel, we don't replace it. We surface owners 6-9 months before they'd call a broker, in the sub-broker-attention tier.

### The one defensible edge, stated plainly

Our durable differentiation is not "we find off-market deals" (everyone claims that). It is **tier + format + transparency**:

- **Tier:** owners with 1-5 properties facing regional-bank or debt-fund loan maturities. LoopNet doesn't have them (unlisted). CRED iQ doesn't have them (only CMBS). Brokers deprioritize them (too small). This specific gap is the entire business.
- **Format:** a curated weekly artifact + on-demand OM analysis, not a database the buyer has to operate.
- **Transparency:** open-source signal methodology and an auditable backtest. The black-box incumbents don't publish theirs.

This edge is narrow and time-boxed (12-24 months while the maturity wall unwinds). We do not need a forever moat. We need to extract value in the window.

### The positioning frame: augment, never replace

Two things we make more powerful, never replace:

**The analyst.** We do the grunt work so the analyst does the judgment work. "Your analyst, supercharged" — never "fire your analyst." The analyst influences or makes the buying decision; threatening their role kills the deal internally. Lead with time given back to the team, not headcount removed from it.

**The broker network.** We feed it, we don't replace it. We surface owners before they'd call a broker, in the sub-broker-attention tier. The syndicator can tip their brokers about what we find. We never position against their relationships.

Both framings are the same underlying move: we are additive to what they already have and trust, not a substitute that creates internal conflict. Every cold message, the landing page, the discovery-call script, and the deliverable copy must pass this test: does it make someone on the client's team feel more powerful, or does it make them feel threatened? If the latter, rewrite it.

The lead pitch reflects this. Not "we replace your analyst" or "we find forced sellers" (commodity, threatening). Instead: "I cut the 2-3 hours your analyst spends on public-records due diligence per OM down to a few minutes, so they can focus on the underwriting only they can do." Specific, believable, positive, and Sentinel **will** deliver this — once we finish hardening it (see Part 18).

### The honest validation rule

This positioning is a hypothesis until tested. The test is not analysis — it is Sentinel in front of real syndicators. Send 20 cold messages offering a free OM analysis. If syndicators forward OMs and ask for more, the pain is real and the product works in the real world. ## Part 18: Sentinel validation status (the honest truth)

### What Sentinel actually is today

Sentinel is a 48-hour hackathon prototype. It is NOT a validated product. The prior versions of this plan implied otherwise — that's been corrected.

What was proven during the hackathon:
- The agent architecture works (ReAct loop, tool orchestration, Nemotron routing/synthesis)
- The skill pipeline completes end-to-end on a handful of demo properties
- The live SSE-streamed UI is compelling as a demo

What was NOT proven (and must be validated before charging a client):
- Whether the pipeline runs reliably on diverse real broker OMs (different formats, lengths, asset types)
- Whether the data pulled from each source (ATTOM, BCAD, CKAN, Census) is accurate and consistent
- Whether the 6-section analysis is actually useful to an experienced multifamily acquisitions team versus generic-sounding
- Whether the system handles edge cases: missing data, scraper failures, weird PDF formats, properties outside Bexar County
- Whether the deliverable PDF looks professional enough to send to a paying client

### The three validation gates Sentinel must pass

Each gate is binary pass/fail. We do not put Sentinel in front of a paying client until all three pass.

**Gate 1 — Reliability:** Sentinel runs the full pipeline successfully on 10 consecutive real broker OMs (sourced from public archives, BiggerPockets, our own cold-outreach replies) without crashing, timing out beyond 3 minutes, or failing more than 1 skill out of 10 per run. Measured by: a run log showing 10 successful end-to-end completions.

**Gate 2 — Data accuracy:** For 10 real properties Sentinel analyzes, we manually verify each fact in the output against ground truth (county records, ATTOM web UI, original OM). The total error rate across all data fields must be under 10%. Critical-signal errors (wrong loan maturity date, wrong owner, wrong hold period) must be under 5%. Measured by: a spreadsheet listing every fact in 10 outputs, marked correct/incorrect, with error rate computed.

**Gate 3 — Usefulness:** At least 3 multifamily-experienced humans (you after the crash course, plus 2 outside reviewers — could be cold-outreach replies, BiggerPockets contributors, or freelance multifamily analysts) rate 5 Sentinel outputs on a 1-10 usefulness scale. Average must be ≥ 7/10, with no single rating below 5/10. Measured by: a feedback document with 15 ratings (3 reviewers × 5 outputs).

### What this means operationally

Until all three gates pass:
- Cold outreach can still happen — but the hook is "I'm building a tool that does X, want to be one of the first to try it free?" — NOT "here's a finished product."
- Free pilot OMs from cold outreach replies feed directly into the validation set. Every prospect who forwards an OM is unknowingly helping us validate.
- No money changes hands until Sentinel passes all three gates AND a prospect explicitly asks how to pay.
- If a prospect wants to pay before validation completes, we accept a deposit but explicitly position it as "founding client pricing on a product still in beta — first 30 days free, then $4K/mo if you decide to continue."

This is the honest sequencing: validate the product while validating the market, in parallel, with the same cold outreach motion feeding both.

### What changes in the offer until Sentinel is validated

The offer shifts during validation. The retainer pitch ($5K setup + $4K/mo) stays as the eventual model but is NOT what we lead with cold. The validation-era offer is:

## Part 19: GTM infrastructure (2026 deliverability and stack)

### The two-domain architecture (non-negotiable in 2026)

Cold email enforcement tightened materially in 2024-2025 (Google/Yahoo Feb 2024, Microsoft May 2025). In 2026, sending cold email from a single primary domain will burn that domain's reputation within 30 days. The standard for any serious cold-email operation is now:

- **Primary brand domain** (e.g., `<yourbrand>.com`): landing page, client communication, transactional emails via Resend, public identity. Never used for cold outreach.
- **Secondary outbound domain** (e.g., `try<yourbrand>.com`, `get<yourbrand>.com`): cold outreach only, after 14-21 days of warmup. Isolates reputation risk from the primary brand.

Both domains require full SPF + DKIM + DMARC authentication. DKIM keys must be 2048-bit minimum (Google rejects 1024-bit signatures to personal Gmail accounts as of 2026). DMARC must start at `p=none` for 30 days while you verify all sending sources are clean, then move to `p=quarantine`. Never start at `p=reject` — it blocks legitimate transactional emails before infrastructure is dialed in.

### Domain warmup (the most-missed step)

A brand-new domain sending cold email on Day 1 is treated by inbox providers as a "new sender with suspicious volume" and routed to spam. Per 2026 deliverability data from MailDeck (1M+ inboxes), Instantly (billions of cold emails), and others:

- Minimum warmup: 14 days. Recommended: 21-30 days.
- Start at 5 emails/day. Ramp by 3-5 emails/day. Cap at 25-30 per mailbox per day total (Gmail throttles bulk senders above this).
- Use a trusted warmup pool: Instantly, Smartlead Premium, or Mailreach. Bad pools burn domains faster than no warmup.
- Keep warmup running in the background at 30-50% of original volume indefinitely after cold sends start. Reputation erodes within 30-60 days if warmup stops.
- Skipping warmup is the #1 documented cause of failed cold email programs in 2026.

### The transition strategy (Weeks 1-2 vs Week 3+)

Because new-domain warmup takes 14-21 days, the immediate solution for getting cold sends out in Week 1 is: **send from your existing personal email** (Gmail or .edu) at low volume (≤5/day) while the secondary outbound domain warms in parallel. Your existing email has years of reputation — sending 5 cold messages/day from it doesn't burn it. By Day 15-21, the warmed secondary domain takes over production cold sends at 10-30/day.

### Landing page (2026 standards)

Per current B2B SaaS benchmarks (median conversion 3.8%, top performers 8-15%):

- **5-second clarity test:** Visitor must understand what you do, who it's for, what action to take within 5 seconds.
- **Headline under 44 characters / 8 words** focused on outcome, not feature.
- **Eyebrow + headline structure:** category line above ("MULTIFAMILY ACQUISITION DECISION SUPPORT") + benefit headline ("Your analyst, supercharged.")
- **One primary CTA above fold.** Multi-CTA pages convert at 10.5% vs single-CTA at 13.5%.
- **Form ≤5 fields.** 5-field forms convert 120% better than longer forms. Our form: email, firm name, target submarket (3 fields).
- **Trust indicators near CTA:** open-source methodology badge, GitHub link, real photo, real bio.
- **Mobile-first.** Tap targets ≥48x48px, body font ≥16px, WCAG AA contrast.
- **Load under 3 seconds.** Lighthouse mobile score >90.
- **Real customer logos when available.** Until then: lead with proof artifacts (GitHub repo, backtest data, Loom demo) instead of fake logos.
